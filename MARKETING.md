# Recap — Marketing Copy

_Working doc for the best tagline, sub-tag, and USPs. Iteration 1._

---

## The product, in one breath

You save articles, newsletters, and PDFs all day meaning to read them — and
never do. Recap finishes the job: overnight, AI reads everything in your queue
(plus any topics you follow, researched live on the web), writes a tight radio
script, and narrates it. You wake up to a ~7-minute podcast of exactly what you
meant to read, waiting in your own private feed.

**Who it's for:** knowledge workers, founders, investors, and the perpetually
curious — people whose "read it later" pile only ever grows, and who already
spend time with earbuds in (commute, gym, dishes, walks).

**The pain:** "read it later" means "read it never." The intention-to-read gap.

**The wedge:** you already save the links. Recap just turns the backlog you'd
feel guilty about into something you'll actually finish — by ear, before coffee.

---

## ★ Recommended

### Tagline
> **Listen to what you meant to read.**

Names the exact emotional gap (intention vs. follow-through) and resolves it in
five words. Pivots cleanly from reading → audio, works without any product
context, and isn't boxed into "news" the way the current line is.

### Sub-tag
> **Save the articles, newsletters, and PDFs you didn't get to. Tomorrow
> morning they're a podcast — scripted and narrated by AI, ready before your
> coffee.**

Spells out the input (your saved reading), the transform (AI script + voice),
and the payoff (a finished listen, on time, effortless).

## Alternate taglines (ranked)

1. **Listen to what you meant to read.** — _recommended; the intention gap._
2. **Your reading list, read to you.** — clean, plays on the double meaning of
   "read"; very clear, slightly less emotional.
3. **Read it later, actually.** — playful jab at the universal failure of
   read-it-later apps; memorable, but leans on in-joke and needs the sub-tag to
   land.
4. **Save it today. Hear it tomorrow.** — crisp, rhythmic, time-anchored; states
   the mechanic but carries less feeling.
5. **Today's news, recapped tomorrow.** — _current line._ Strong rhythm and
   ties to the name, but frames Recap as a news app when the real hook is *your*
   saved reading, not headlines.

### Alternate sub-tags
- _For #2:_ "Drop in the links you saved today. Wake up to an AI-narrated
  podcast that reads them back to you — about seven minutes, just yours."
- _For #4:_ "Queue up what you didn't get to. Overnight, AI turns it into a
  short, natural-sounding briefing waiting in your podcast app by morning."

## USPs (unique selling points)

Lead with **1–3** on the page; the rest are supporting bullets.

1. **Your backlog becomes a daily listen.**
   The read-it-later pile you never finish turns into one ~7-minute podcast —
   five stories, read back to you. You already saved the links; we read them.

2. **Written for radio, not bullet points.**
   Claude scripts each piece into natural narration that actually flows, then
   reads it in a clear voice. No robotic "summary of summaries."

3. **Ready before your coffee.**
   Set your hour and timezone; your recap lands right when you wake — no app to
   open, no "catch up later."

4. **Follow topics, not just links.**
   Pick subjects like World news or Technology — or write your own — and we
   research them live on the web. You get a brief even on days you saved nothing.

5. **Lives in the podcast app you already use.**
   A private feed for Apple Podcasts, Overcast, or Pocket Casts. New recaps
   appear automatically — no new app to learn.

6. **Private by default.**
   Your queue and feed are yours alone, secured by a secret link you can rotate
   any time.

## Positioning note

The current site leans on "today's news." The product's sharper, more defensible
angle is **your own saved reading** — the hero mockup already shows Stratechery,
a macro PDF, and a newsletter, not breaking headlines. Recap isn't another news
feed; it's the thing that finally clears the pile you curated yourself. The
recommended copy leans into that wedge.
